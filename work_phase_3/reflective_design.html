Reflective Design

The focus of this section of the Style Guide is ensuring the user has a good first impression of the site.

I have divided this style guide into three sections based on Don Norman‚Äôs Emotional Design Levels.

This table links the core values identified for the client and community to design implications and specific elements of the site. Each value is annotated to explain why it matters and how it influences the design.

Value Design Implication Specific Element
Belonging Show diversity and inclusion to reflect community identity. Homepage hero image with diverse community members.
Trust Provide clarity and transparency to encourage safe interaction. Clear consent text in newsletter subscription form.
Accessibility Ensure inclusivity across different abilities and contexts. High contrast text, alt text for all images, keyboard navigation.
Sustainability Promote eco-friendly practices and long-term community benefit. Icons and messaging that highlight sustainability initiatives.
Engagement Encourage participation and interaction in the community. Interactive cards, call-to-action buttons, and event sign-up forms.

Annotations

Belonging: Central to reducing social isolation. Diverse community images help users feel represented.

Trust: Transparency aligns with ethical design principles (ACS Code) and builds confidence in site use.

Accessibility: Designing for all ensures compliance with WCAG 2.2 and supports inclusive use.

Sustainability: Reflecting eco-friendly practices builds alignment with community and global values.

Engagement: Clear calls-to-action motivate users to participate actively, increasing site usefulness.

Imagery and Tone

This section outlines the intended identity and emotional tone of the website. The imagery and language choices are aligned to ensure a consistent reflective design.

Identity

The website is designed to project an identity of trust, belonging, and optimism, ensuring visitors feel welcomed and represented.

Emotional Tone

The tone is friendly, inclusive, and supportive, encouraging users to feel safe and motivated to engage with the community.

Annotation

The combination of imagery and tone reinforces the client's mission to build a safe, inclusive, and engaging community. The selected visuals and language ensure that users can see themselves represented and feel emotionally connected to the site.

Language and Expression

This section documents our inclusive language checklist, a draft homepage text, a revised version aligned to the checklist and WCAG 3.1.1 (Readable), and annotations explaining key changes.

Inclusive Language Checklist

  • Avoid labels like ‚Äúthe elderly‚Äù; use ‚Äúolder people‚Äù.
  • Use person-first phrasing: ‚Äúperson with a disability‚Äù.
  • Use plain English; explain jargon on first use.
